In January 2023, experts predicted the emergence of advanced models like GPT-4 within two years, but it appeared after just two months. In December 2024, analysts expected 30% improvements in 2026 models, yet some benchmarks saw 400% improvements. This analysis relies on Gartner, McKinsey, and World Economic Forum reports from 2025-2026, focusing on the rapid transformation brought by the intelligent agents revolution.

### From Science Fiction to Technical Reality

Before 2017, when AI was still in limited experimental stages, Hollywood films presented scenarios that seemed completely fantastical. In the 2008 film "Iron Man," the hero Tony Stark talks with JARVIS, an intelligent voice assistant that understands natural commands and executes complex multi-step tasks like data analysis, device control, and even proposing strategic solutions in real time. The 2013 film "Her," set in 2025, presents Samantha, an intelligent operating system that interacts vocally with the user, learns their personality, builds an emotional relationship, and performs daily tasks autonomously. Audiences watched these scenes in awe, considering them far-fetched.

Today, after the intelligent agents revolution, these scenarios have become technical reality. Agents are no longer just tools responding to queries; they're systems capable of planning, execution, and adaptation to multi-step tasks without continuous human intervention. Gartner's August 2025 report confirms that 40% of enterprise applications will contain intelligent agents specialized in specific tasks by the end of 2026, compared to less than 5% in 2025. This rapid spread reflects agents' ability to complete research, analysis, formulation, and full execution of tasks, transforming AI from an assistant tool to a practical partner that minimizes errors in well-defined contexts.

### 2026-2030: Spread of Autonomous Agents Across Vital Domains

Intelligent agents have begun direct integration with daily systems like Outlook, Slack, and Notion, where they accomplish multi-step tasks. Gartner's 2025 report indicates that 70% of companies will use intelligent agents in IT infrastructure operations by 2029, compared to less than 5% in 2025. In transportation, self-driving cars are advancing at a notable pace. Goldman Sachs estimates suggest that 10% of new car sales globally will be Level 3 (allowing drivers to look away from the road under specific conditions) by 2030, while full self-driving vehicles may reach 2.5%. By 2035, the sector could generate revenues between $300 and $400 billion, according to McKinsey, with mobility transforming into a shared service relying on automated fleets that reduce congestion and improve safety.

In precision medicine, AI contributes to expanding the market from $3.97 billion currently to $125.81 billion by 2040, with a 28% compound annual growth rate, according to specialized market reports. Digital twins and sensors now enable continuous predictive diagnosis, while AlphaFold 3 (which solved the protein folding problem in 2024) allows discovery of new drugs in weeks instead of years. Screening and analysis have become highly automated, though final diagnosis remains under human responsibility for legal reasons.

Regarding the labor market, the World Economic Forum's 2025 report (Future of Jobs Report) predicts that AI will displace 92 million jobs by 2030, while creating 170 million new jobs, resulting in a net gain of 78 million jobs. The fastest-growing jobs include big data specialists, fintech engineers, and AI/machine learning specialists. Simultaneously, 30-70% of office tasks in some sectors are automated, according to McKinsey, with agents focusing on routine tasks like claims processing in insurance or scheduling meetings through multi-agent systems.

### 2030-2035: Restructuring Labor Markets and Daily Life

During this period, the focus shifts to deep integration between humans and AI. McKinsey's report indicates AI could generate economic value reaching $15.7-23 trillion annually by 2040, focusing on partnerships between humans, agents, and robots. In daily life, personal agents become independent assistants managing schedules, purchasing goods, and analyzing health data from wearable sensors. Multi-agent systems, like those used in insurance claims processing (where seven specialized agents collaborate in planning, fraud verification, and payment), expand to other sectors like e-commerce and financial services.

In education, AI enables personalized learning programs based on real-time individual performance analysis, while in entertainment it offers interactive content that adapts to user preferences. The safest jobs remain those requiring physical presence, complex human relationships, or strategic judgment. Gartner predicts the cost of large language model inference will drop by 90% by 2030, making agents widely accessible.

### 2035-2040: Artificial General Intelligence and Geopolitical Transformations

Expert surveys from AI Impacts (2025-2026) give a 50% probability of achieving Artificial General Intelligence between 2040 and 2050. Current indicators show rapid improvement in specialized tasks, but general intelligence requires solutions for efficiency, costs, and alignment with human values. In medicine, the drug discovery market continues growing from $1.81 billion to $41.08 billion (25% growth rate), with continuous predictive diagnosis through digital twins.

Geopolitically, the United States advances in fundamental innovation, while China excels in large-scale application and manufacturing. The LEAP survey (2026) expects the gap between American and Chinese models to shrink by 2030 and reach parity by 2040. For Arab countries, reliance on open-source models and local development remains a critical factor for digital sovereignty.

**2035-2040:** Continuous learning and adaptation to a multipolar world, and ability to direct groups of agents.

Every previous technological revolution created jobs and welfare that were unimaginable, but the transition was painful in some aspects. AI until 2040 represents a massive transformative force. How to direct this force — for whom, with what values, and under what oversight — are questions that transcend the technical realm to include policies, institutions, and collective decisions, and remain open to unexpected developments.
